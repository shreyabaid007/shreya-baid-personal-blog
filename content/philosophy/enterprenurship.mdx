Solopreneurship is rising rapidly in tech because AI has reduced the cost of building, which means more individuals can now operate independently.

This is progress in capability, but capability does not change intent, it only amplifies it.

So the real question is simple: has entrepreneurship evolved in purpose, or is it still driven by the same primitive goal of more profit and more financial success?

One could argue that profit reflects value creation, which is valid, but it quietly replaces the question “what is valuable?” with “what can be sold.”

If the system defines value, and we never question the system, then we are not thinking, we are complying.

Now look at how desire evolves.

At first, we collect things. Then we collect approval. Then we collect money. Then we collect symbols of success.

At every stage, we call it growth, but we are still just collecting.

If the structure is unchanged, then the person has not fundamentally changed; only the context has.

So when the same individual becomes an entrepreneur, why assume the intention has suddenly become deeper?

Martin Heidegger described this as Das Man, living through borrowed norms, where one believes they are choosing, but are only repeating what “one does.”

In that sense, chasing valuation or exit may not be ambition, it may be conformity refined and rewarded.

There is nothing wrong with wanting more.

The problem begins when one does not know why they want it, because what is unexamined is rarely one’s own.

And what is not one’s own cannot sustain commitment.

So the pattern follows.

The outcome is fixed first, profit, scale, exit.

Then purpose is shaped around it.

When the destination is borrowed, the journey becomes mechanical.

And mechanical effort cannot produce meaningful attachment.

This is why commitment remains conditional.

If results take longer than expected, the venture is abandoned, not because it is wrong, but because it is slow.

So failure is redefined as delay.

Speed becomes a substitute for truth.

From the same logic, exits are celebrated.

But what is an exit? It is the moment one is free from what one built.

If freedom is the reward, then the work was a burden. And if the work was a burden, then it was never loved.

Yet this is glorified, because the outcome is money.

Imagine an employee celebrating resignation as their great achievement, it would seem misaligned.

Yet when founders do the same, it is admired.

The intent is identical, only the story is different.

There is nothing wrong with chasing money. But why is it rarely stated directly?

Because once something is glorified, it becomes unquestionable.

This is where the real cost appears, especially for the younger generation.

They do not inherit clarity, they inherit imitation. They see outcomes and assume those outcomes define meaning.

So they stop asking what they truly want to do. Instead, they ask what has worked before.

And in that moment, intelligence is replaced by repetition.

**A different path begins with a different question. Not what will succeed, but what is worth doing.**

This question is harder because it offers no immediate metric.

But it creates alignment, because the direction is internally chosen.

From there, money and capital still matter, but only as tools.

In such a case, exits are no longer the goal, because the work is not something to escape from.

And entrepreneurship stops being a pursuit of more and becomes a pursuit of clarity.

Which is far less glamorous; but far more demanding.

Because tools can reduce effort, markets can reward outcomes, but no system can answer one question for you:

Is this truly what you want to build or just what you have learned to want?
