# Embedding Privacy by Design in Multimodal AI Development

Multimodal AI systems — models that process text, images, audio, and video together — handle far more personal data than single-input models. A user's voice, face, and typed query can all be processed in a single interaction. That broader data surface makes GDPR compliance harder, and the stakes of getting it wrong significantly higher.

The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) published two key documents in late 2024 and early 2025 that directly address this: **Opinion 28/2024 on AI Models** and **Guidelines 01/2025 on Pseudonymisation**. This article breaks down what they require, and what AI developers actually need to do.

---

## 1. What the EDPB Guidelines Cover

### 1.1 The EDPB's Role in AI Regulation

The EDPB enforces GDPR principles as AI technology evolves. Rather than slowing innovation, its guidelines are meant to create a clear legal path — so developers know what's permissible, what requires justification, and what isn't allowed at all.

Opinion 28/2024 specifically addresses three questions that came directly from the Irish supervisory authority:

1. When can an AI model be considered **anonymous**?
2. How can controllers justify **legitimate interest** as a legal basis during development?
3. What happens when personal data is **unlawfully processed** during training — does it taint the deployed model?

### 1.2 Key Concepts at a Glance

| Concept | What It Means |
|--------|----------------|
| **Pseudonymisation** | Replaces identifiers with pseudonyms — data stays useful but less exposed. Still personal data under GDPR. |
| **Anonymisation** | Permanently removes all identifiability — GDPR no longer applies if done correctly. |
| **Privacy by Design** | Data protection built into the system from day one, not bolted on after. |
| **Privacy by Default** | Systems process only the minimum data necessary, by default, without user action. |

## 2. Privacy by Design in AI Development

### 2.1 Data Collection and Preparation

Before any model is trained, the data pipeline itself needs to be GDPR-compliant. That means:

- **Data Minimisation** — Only collect what the model genuinely needs. If a multimodal model doesn't need real faces to function, don't train it on them.
- **Transparency** — Users must be told how their data is being used, in plain terms.
- **Meaningful Consent** — Consent must be a real choice, not buried in terms of service.

### 2.2 Privacy-Preserving Techniques

These are the technical tools the EDPB recognises as effective during model development:

| Technique | How It Helps |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Pseudonymisation** | Masks identities in training data while keeping it analytically useful |
| **Synthetic Data Generation** | Generates artificial data that mirrors real patterns — no actual individuals involved |
| **Differential Privacy** | Adds calibrated statistical noise so individual data points can't be extracted |

### 2.3 Risk Assessment — The DPIA

A **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)** is not optional for high-risk AI processing — it's required. It should identify:

- Risk of **data linkage** — where combining datasets re-identifies individuals
- Risk of **unauthorised access** during training or storage
- Gaps in **encryption and access controls**

The DPIA should be a living document, revisited as the model evolves — not a one-time checkbox.

---

## 3. When Can an AI Model Itself Be Considered Anonymous?

This is one of the most practically important questions Opinion 28/2024 addresses — and the answer is more cautious than many developers expect.

The EDPB states that **AI models trained on personal data cannot automatically be assumed anonymous**. For a model to be considered anonymous, both of the following conditions must be met:

1. The likelihood of **directly extracting personal data** from the model — including through probabilistic methods — must be insignificant.
2. The likelihood of **obtaining personal data through queries** — intentionally or not — must also be insignificant.

This matters practically: a language model that was trained on personal data could still reproduce or leak that data through its outputs. The EDPB expects controllers to actively demonstrate anonymity — through documentation, technical testing, and resistance to known extraction attacks — not simply assert it.

## 4. Privacy by Design in AI Deployment

### 4.1 Transparency and Accountability

Once a model is deployed, GDPR obligations don't stop. Controllers must:

- Keep detailed **records of data processing activities**
- Ensure the AI is **explainable** — users affected by AI decisions should be able to understand why
- Maintain clear **accountability** — someone is always responsible for data governance

### 4.2 Data Subject Rights

AI systems must be built to support these rights technically, not just acknowledge them in policy:

| Right | What the System Must Support |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **Access & Correction** | Users can view and amend their data |
| **Erasure & Portability** | Users can delete their data or export it |
| **Consent Withdrawal** | Users can withdraw consent simply, without friction |

## 5. The Three Core Techniques for Multimodal AI

### 5.1 Anonymisation

True anonymisation means an individual cannot be **singled out**, **linked across datasets**, or **inferred** — even when the anonymised data is combined with external information.

If those conditions are genuinely met, the data falls outside GDPR's scope entirely, which significantly simplifies lawful processing. The catch: the EDPB holds this to a high standard, and the burden of proof sits with the controller.

---

### 5.2 Pseudonymisation

Pseudonymisation replaces direct identifiers with pseudonyms. The original data can still be recovered using separately stored additional information — which is why it remains personal data under GDPR.

> ⚠️ Pseudonymised data still requires a valid legal basis, transparency obligations, and data protection compliance — it doesn't reduce your GDPR obligations, it reduces your risk exposure.

Per Guidelines 01/2025, pseudonymisation is most effective when the **pseudonymisation domain** is tightly defined — meaning only authorised recipients with a specific purpose can access the additional information needed to re-link the data. The broader that circle, the weaker the protection.

---

### 5.3 Synthetic Data

Synthetic data is generated to replicate the **statistical patterns** of real data, without representing any actual individual. For multimodal AI, this is especially valuable — synthetic faces, synthetic voices, and synthetic text can stand in for real training data.

The EDPB requires a **Privacy Assurance Assessment** before relying on synthetic data, to confirm:
- The data cannot be used to re-identify real individuals
- No traces of personal data from the original dataset persist in the synthetic output

---

## Summary: Techniques at a Glance

| Technique | GDPR Applies? | Re-ID Risk | Best Used For |
|-----------|--------------|------------|---------------|
| **Anonymisation** | No (if correctly done) | None | Data sharing, open datasets |
| **Pseudonymisation** | Yes | Controlled | Model development, internal processing |
| **Synthetic Data** | Depends on method | Low (if validated) | AI training, testing pipelines |
| **Differential Privacy** | Yes | Very low | Statistical analysis, ML training |
